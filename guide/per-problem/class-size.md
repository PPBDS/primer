### Class Size  *(standalone · Medium · one scenario: causal)*

- **Type:** example (class exercise only)
- **Renderings:** chapter — · tutorial — · class exercise ✓ (`primer.exercises` repo, `class-size/`)
- **"Imagine" (one, given to students verbatim — reduced from two scenarios to the causal one alone, 2026-07-15, because considering two Imagines is too much for students):** "Imagine you work for the Texas Department of Education. You want to understand the effect of small classes on student performance in Dallas. In Texas, there is data available like the data in the STAR project."
- **Preceptor's Question:** What is the average causal effect of assigning a Dallas student to a small class on their fourth-grade math score? (Control deliberately unspecified — regular and regular-with-aide are both live controls, so there is more than one causal effect to discuss.)
- **Dataset:** `data/class-size.csv` — 6,325 students from the Tennessee STAR experiment (Mosteller 1995; data cleaned and discussed in Imai, *Quantitative Social Science*). Students were randomly assigned to small (13–17), regular (22–25), or regular-with-aide classes in grades K–3. Columns: `race`, `classtype` (1/2/3 — the **kindergarten** assignment, randomized at school entry), `yearssmall` (0–4 years spent in small classes across K–3), `hsgrad`, `g4math` (grade-4 **math computation** scale score — `g4mathcomputss` in the STAR archive, *not* the total math score `g4tmathss`), `g4reading` (reading comprehension subscore, `g4readcomprehss`). **3,930 of 6,325 students (62%) are missing `g4math`** — balanced across class types (61.1–63.6%) but a first-order data-quality discussion. *Provenance verified 2026-07-13:* byte-identical to `STAR.csv` in Imai's QSS repo, and row-identical to the kindergarten entrants in the primary Project STAR archive (Harvard Dataverse doi:10.7910/DVN/SIWH9F, `STAR_Students`).
- **Underlying paper:** Mosteller (1995), "The Tennessee study of class size in the early school grades," *The Future of Children* 5(2) — in the folder as `tennessee-star.pdf` (recovered via the Wayback Machine from EdSource's copy).
- **Outcome:** `g4math` — score on the math computation portion of the fourth-grade Stanford Achievement Test (continuous; mean ≈ 709, sd ≈ 43)
- **Treatment:** the **kindergarten** class-type assignment (small / regular / regular with aid), randomized at school entry. Three levels → three potential outcomes.
- **Covariates:** `race` (recoded White/Black/Others), `yearssmall` (dose: years spent in small classes)
- **Causal / Predictive:** causal.
- **Data prep (in the setup chunk of every file — students receive it, don't write it):** read CSV → recode `classtype` 1/2/3 to `kind` (small / regular / regular with aid) → recode `race` 1=White, 2=Black, 3–6=Others → `select(g4math, kind, race, yearssmall)` → object `star`.
- **Candidate models (Courage):** `g4math ~ kind` → `fit_kind` (nothing distinguishable); `g4math ~ kind + race` → `fit_kind_race` (race matters, kind still nothing); `+ yearssmall` → `fit_kind_race_years`.
- **Final model:** `linear_reg() |> fit(g4math ~ kind + race + yearssmall, data = star)` → `fit_classsize`. Equation: `695 − 1.95·regular_with_aid − 7.66·small + 38.7·Others + 16.2·White + 2.18·yearssmall`.
- **Headline numbers:** average predicted scores by kind 710 / 708 / 709 (heavily overlapping CIs); `avg_comparisons` small − regular −7.66 [−15.9, 0.6] (includes zero); **`yearssmall` +2.18 [0.10, 4.27] per year** — the dose, not the snapshot assignment, is where the signal is. This near-null teaching story is the point: a famous experiment whose headline effect is invisible in this simple cut.
- **Preceptor Table:** Unit (Student) | Potential Outcomes (Score if Small, Score if Regular, Score if Regular with Aide — three levels, two hatched per row) | Treatment (Class Assignment)
- **Population Table:** same columns plus Source and Unit/Time (Student, Year). Data rows: Tennessee, 1985. Preceptor rows: Dallas students now in K–3, whose fourth-grade scores arrive 2027–2030 — all four cohorts.
- **Assumption hooks:** validity — different math tests and different definitions of "small" in Dallas today vs Tennessee 1985. Stability — home lives, curricula, and teaching have changed since the 1980s. Representativeness — 1980s Tennessee volunteer schools are not a draw from today's Dallas. Unconfoundedness — randomized by design, but was randomization executed cleanly (administrators wanting their own kids in small classes)? Missing-outcome data (62%) threatens everything if missingness is not random.
